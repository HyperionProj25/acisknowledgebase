**The short version:** ACIS has two validation results, and they answer two different
questions. Keep them separate. One measures how well the system tells arms that broke
down apart from arms that did not (discrimination, reported as AUC). The other measures
how many injured pitchers it flagged in time (catch rate). Fusing them is the most common
mistake people make with these numbers.

---

## Study 1: Blind-cohort discrimination (the headline)

This is the current headline result and the framing all outbound materials use.

SP12 scored a **<Fact k="validation.cohort" /> cohort of <Fact k="validation.cohortSize" /> arms**: **<Fact k="validation.cases" />** that
landed on the injured list with an arm injury and **<Fact k="validation.controls" />** workload-matched healthy
controls, drawn from 2023 to 2025, sourced from the MLB Stats API, and never seen in
training. "Held-out" means these arms were excluded from the model's training data, so it
scored them cold. "Workload-matched" means the healthy controls were matched to the
injured arms on workload, so the comparison is not just "busy pitchers versus idle ones."

- **AUC <Fact k="validation.auc" />**, up from **<Fact k="validation.priorAuc" />** on the prior model (the "Original Model") on the same
  cohort. AUC of 0.80 means that if you draw one injured arm and one healthy arm at
  random, the model gives the injured one a higher score about four times out of five.
- **Sustained elevation is the signal, not a single spike.** The thing that separates the
  two groups is the *share* of a pitcher's starts sitting at or above the flag line, not
  whether they crossed it once. Single-crossing detection saturates near 100% sensitivity
  at almost zero specificity, so it was retired in favor of sustained elevation.
- **The signal is invisible to the box score.** Within-pitcher correlation between the
  ACIS score and Game Score, strikeout rate, and walk rate is **<Fact k="validation.boxScoreCorr" />**.
  This is the "why your performance analytics cannot already see this" argument.
- **It survives the season-timing test.** SP12 **<Fact k="validation.earlySeason" />**.
  It is reading the arm, not the calendar.
- **Honest operating point:** at the balanced threshold, **<Fact k="validation.operatingPoint" />**,
  which means about one in three events is still missed. The operating point was tuned
  in-sample and should be confirmed on a future season.

## Study 2: In-season catch rate (prior-model era)

Internal history. This is the earlier catch-rate framing, retired from outbound materials
in favor of the 252-cohort AUC result above. It is documented here because it is real, it
is the ground-truth lens the champion was built against, and the injury-type breakdown
below is still the clearest evidence that the signal is biomechanical rather than noise.

**One numerator, two denominators.** This is the part people get wrong:

- **<Fact k="priorStudy.catches" /> catches** = injured pitchers flagged at or above the threshold before their IL date.
- **In-season rate = <Fact k="priorStudy.catches" /> / 156 = <Fact k="priorStudy.inSeason" />.** The denominator excludes cases ACIS had no
  regular-season data to score.
- **Raw rate = <Fact k="priorStudy.catches" /> / <Fact k="priorStudy.pool" /> = <Fact k="priorStudy.raw" />.** The denominator includes all <Fact k="priorStudy.pool" /> injured cases, even
  the **22** with zero in-season data (spring-training or pre-season IL). So 178 minus 156
  equals 22 unscoreable cases. <Fact k="priorStudy.inSeason" /> is the operating-window number; <Fact k="priorStudy.raw" /> is the floor.

**Definitions (locked, pre-registered):**

- **Threshold of record:** a score at or above **41** on the 0 to 100 scale counts as a flag.
- **Catch:** flagged at any valid start before the IL date.
- **Valid window:** begins at each pitcher's **5th start** (the chronic-workload baseline
  needs roughly four starts to form) and runs through the first 65% of the season. Reads
  before the 5th start are pre-baseline and are never counted as misses.

**Catch rate by injury type (the hard-to-fake result).** This uses a stricter, smaller
pool of **77** verified-injured pitchers with a recorded injury type (2023 and 2025; 2024
is excluded because injury type was not recorded that season). Do not try to reconcile 77
against the 178 headline. They are different cuts with different inclusion rules. After
removing operating-window artifacts, ACIS caught **100% of scoreable UCL / Tommy John**
cases and **100% of scoreable flexor / forearm** cases, the canonical cumulative-overuse
medial-elbow injuries. The gradient (high on workload-mediated elbow injuries, low on
acute and non-elbow ones) is what argues against a "this is just a velocity proxy"
explanation.

**Genuine misses.** Across 2023 and 2025 there were **3** genuine misses (cases with valid
in-window data that still were not flagged): an acute bone bruise, a teres strain, and a
shoulder strain. None were workload-mediated medial-elbow injuries. The misses landing in
non-cumulative mechanisms is itself evidence for the fatigue thesis.

## Soft spots (state these before an analyst finds them)

- **Specificity is the weak point.** Pooled specificity is **46.9%**, and **29.6%** in
  2025. The system over-flags healthy pitchers. Never present catch rate as a standalone
  headline; always pair it with specificity and confidence intervals.
- **Acute strains are out of the wheelhouse.** Lat and teres catch rate is low (29% raw,
  50% after removing artifacts). These are acute events, not cumulative fatigue. Volunteer it.
- **A 41 to 69 gray zone exists** where injured and healthy score distributions overlap.
  Magnitude alone cannot separate arms in that band.
- **Season-progress calendar artifact.** The season-progress feature inflates scores early
  and collapses them late, independent of arm health. The 5th-start-to-65% valid window is
  what contains it, which is why scoring outside that window is out of scope.
- **Do not trust the engine's text labels.** The engine's risk-level labels do not match
  the published five-tier legend. Always analyze by the score number.

## How the two studies relate (provenance)

- **Research corpus** (model development): **<Fact k="corpus.pitches" />** tracked pitches across **11 MLB
  seasons** plus AAA, about **<Fact k="corpus.appearances" />** pitcher-game appearances, evaluated with
  leave-season-out cross-validation. This produces an out-of-fold AUC of about **0.72**.
  This is the development number.
- **Blind validation cohort** (out-of-sample test): the **<Fact k="validation.cohortSize" />** held-out arms above,
  AUC **<Fact k="validation.auc" />**. This is the validation number.

Different magnitudes, different jobs. The corpus tells you how the model was built and
tuned. The cohort tells you how it did on arms it had never seen.
