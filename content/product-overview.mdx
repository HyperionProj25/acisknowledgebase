**ACIS is a post-game arm-fatigue and workload-intelligence system for MLB pitchers.** After
each start, it scores the arm from 0 to 100 based on how the pitcher's recent workload and
performance signals compare to the state that precedes breakdown. One score per appearance,
mapped to a five-tier scale.

## The problem it addresses

Pitcher arm injuries are one of the largest unsolved cost and product-quality problems in
MLB. Teams lose premium salary to the injured list every season, and the sport loses its
best arms to breakdowns that build up quietly across a season. The signal that precedes those
breakdowns is real but hard to see with the box score, which is the gap ACIS fills.

sustained elevation across starts, and it is roughly uncorrelated with box-score performance,
which is why conventional analytics do not already surface it. See [Validation](/validation)
for the full result and its limits.

## What ACIS is not

- **Not real-time.** It is a post-game read, never described as an in-game or live system.
- **Not an injury predictor.** {/* lint-language-disable-line: states the ban */} It is a
  fatigue meter. It does not output a probability of injury, and we do not frame it that way.
- **Not a data moat.** ACIS runs on public Statcast data. The company does not have, and does
  not claim, a proprietary-data advantage. This matters; see [GTM Rails](/gtm-rails) before
  writing anything external.

## Where the forward edge actually is

The durable advantage is not historical data. It is two things: access to biomechanical data
(Hawk-Eye and TrackMan) that raises the ceiling on what the model can see, and distribution
partnerships that put ACIS in front of teams. The biomechanical research direction is laid out
in the [Biomechanics Roadmap](/biomechanics-roadmap); partnership status is tracked in
[GTM Rails](/gtm-rails).
