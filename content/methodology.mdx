**The short version:** ACIS is a post-game arm-fatigue index. After each start, it reads a
pitcher's recent workload and performance-decay signals and returns a 0 to 100 score
trained against a 30-day arm-injury-list outcome. It is not a real-time feed and not a
probability of injury. It is a fatigue meter.

## What the model is

The scoring champion is **<Fact k="internal.champion" />**, a gradient-boosted tree model (**XGBoost**). It is trained
on the research corpus (**<Fact k="corpus.pitches" />** tracked pitches, **<Fact k="corpus.appearances" />** pitcher-game appearances across
11 MLB seasons plus AAA) and produces one score per appearance. The learning target is
whether an arm reached the injured list with an arm injury within a 30-day horizon, so the
model learns the *state that precedes* breakdown rather than any single event.

## What it reads (feature families)

The documented champion fields fall into three families. Exact per-feature definitions are
in the [Data Dictionary](/data-dictionary).

- **Workload accumulation.** Acute and chronic load, the acute-to-chronic workload ratio
  (ACWR), a fatigue-pressure composite, rest days, and season-to-date volume (games,
  pitches, season progress).
- **Performance decay.** Fastball velocity trend (VeloSlope), spin trend (SpinSlope), and
  average fastball velocity. These are the biomechanical proxies: an arm compensating for
  fatigue tends to bleed velocity and spin before it fails.
- **Injury history.** Prior arm-IL count and prior Tommy John status.

The design rationale for these choices (why exponentially-weighted workload beats a raw
ACWR window, why velocity-decay slope and release-point drift are load-bearing) is
documented in the pitcher-fatigue research report. Note that report describes the feature
*basis*; the authoritative production feature set is defined in the Data Dictionary.

## How it explains itself

Every score ships with its top drivers via **SHAP** (the standard method for attributing a
tree model's output to individual features). Operationally the read surfaces the **top 3
drivers** per appearance. The most common are <Fact k="internal.topDrivers" />. A full
top-10 SHAP-to-mechanism mapping is a roadmap item, not yet shipped, so do not present it
as live.

## How it was validated

- **Development:** leave-season-out cross-validation. The model is trained on all seasons
  but one and tested on the held-out season, rotating through. This prevents a model from
  looking good only because it memorized a particular year. Out-of-fold AUC is about 0.72.
- **Out-of-sample:** the blind <Fact k="validation.cohortSize" />-arm cohort described in
  [Validation](/validation), AUC <Fact k="validation.auc" />. See the Validation section
  for the full result, the operating point, and the soft spots.

## What it outputs

- A **<Fact k="internal.scale" />** fatigue score, mapped to a five-tier scale (Low 0-20, Moderate 21-40,
  Elevated 41-60, High 61-80, Critical 81-100).
- A flag at a score of **<Fact k="internal.flagThreshold" />** or higher (Elevated and above).
- A valid scoring window from a pitcher's 5th start (the chronic baseline needs about four
  starts to form) through the first 65% of the season. Reads outside that window are out of
  scope, not misses.

## Scope and language

ACIS is a **post-game** system, never described as real-time or in-game. It is a fatigue
and workload-intelligence tool, full stop: the banned-framing rules in
[GTM Rails](/gtm-rails) and CLAUDE.md govern how it is described, and the CI language lint
enforces them on every build. The signal that carries the model is *sustained elevation*
across starts, not any single reading.
